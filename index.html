Computer Science + Learning Sciences

I'm a second-year PhD Student and NSF CSGrad4US Fellow at Northwestern University, advised by Dr. Eleanor O'Rourke. In my work, I focus on post-secondary Computer Science students and how they navigate the transition from academia to industry through things like internships, technical interview preparation, and other professional development practices.
 
By understanding how students currently bridge this gap, I aim to design curriculum interventions that formalize and streamline their path‚Äîbringing industry practices and innovations back into the classroom.
 
I'm also passionate about equipping students with skills AI can't replace: self-regulation, metacognition, and self-efficacy.

About

The MoraL Code
Hi there, I'm Mora Labisi and I put the "Mora L" in The MoraL Code! As a child, my dad used my room as a storage space for his many PCs that he liked to tinker with when he had the time. As a result, I looked to the computer for entertainment, which in turn led to me creating webpages about dogs and making simple computer games. Despite my early exposure to the field, I never actually considered CS as a career. It was always simply a fun hobby for me‚Äîtweaking Tumblr themes in HTML/CSS and keeping up with advancements in tech. In fact, the idea of CS as a career didn't really hit me until I was applying for college back in 2015. I applied to Cal Poly Pomona's CS program, self-studied Java, and never looked back! After graduating from Cal Poly Pomona with my Bachelor's Degree in CS, I spent a little over four years crafting code and systems as a Software Engineer at Starbucks.
The MoraL Code
CS students learn algorithms and data structures in the classroom, but professional programming requires a different set of skills: problem-solving under pressure, communicating your thought process, and navigating ambiguous questions. Many students bridge this gap on their own through LeetCode grinding, side projects, and mock interviews. My research asks: what if we formalized this? What would it look like to integrate industry-readiness into the curriculum instead of leaving students to figure it out alone?
